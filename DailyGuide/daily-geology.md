# Daily Geology

This guide describes the geology encountered along the Philmont Trek 12-15 route.
It is based on the USGS geology reference `References/USGS/ofr_22.pdf`, the route map in `References/USGS/USGS_I-425_1-prnt.pdf`, and the attached trail route screenshot.

## Regional Geology Overview
- The Philmont trek sits on the eastern slopes of the Cimarron Range, with the easternmost ranch area grading into the Great Plains.
- The low foothills and valley floors are underlain by soft Cretaceous Pierre shale and related sedimentary rocks.
- To the south, higher mesas are capped by basalt flows that rest on Cretaceous shales.
- Near the core of the Cimarron Range, the geology transitions into older, resistant crystalline rocks: amphibolites, hornblende schists, and pink to reddish granitic pegmatites.
- Quartz-monzonite porphyry dikes cut the shale country and form steep, resistant ridges in the middle portion of the ranch.

## Day 0 — Travel to Philmont
- Arrival at Camping Headquarters introduces the low-elevation foothill geology of the ranch.
- This area is dominated by gently rolling hills underlain by Cretaceous shale, the same kind of soft, gray-brown sedimentary rock that forms the eastern plains.
- The nearby map shows the route beginning in the broad valley that leads toward Cimarroncito Creek and the mountain front.

## Day 1 — Base Camp
- Base Camp sits near the gentle eastern flank of the Cimarron Range.
- Hikers begin where the Great Plains transition into the low ridges of the ranch, with terrain underlain by Pierre shale and other Cretaceous mudrocks.
- Early views are of shale-dominated slopes, light-colored stream valleys, and the rising foreland of the forested mountains ahead.

## Day 2 — Base Camp to Harlan
- The route starts at Turkey Creek Trailhead and climbs into the lower pined ridge country.
- As you move westward, the shale foothills steepen and begin to approach the more resistant structural features of the range.
- Harlan at 7,550 ft lies near the lower flank of the Cimarron Range, where east-dipping Cretaceous strata are succeeded by the first signs of intrusive dike influence.

## Day 3 — Harlan to Devil's Wash Basin
- This day crosses higher forested ridges that are shaped by Cretaceous shale and sandstone layers.
- The attached trail map shows the route running through the central part of the ranch, where north-south porphyry dikes create sharper ridgelines and cliff-forming outcrops.
- Devil's Wash Basin sits above the softer shale terrain, and the basin landscape may expose late Carboniferous–age redbeds and conglomerates below the forest cover.

## Day 4 — Devil's Wash Basin to Cimarron River
- Descend from the wash basin toward the Cimarron River corridor.
- The river valley is cut into soft Cretaceous Pierre shale and valley alluvium, creating a marked contrast with the dike-influenced ridges nearby.
- Along this march, hikers can observe the shift from upland shale slopes into the river-carved sedimentary valley.

## Day 5 — Cimarron River to Santa Claus
- Santa Claus dry camp lies on high prairie country underlain by shale and related sedimentary rocks.
- The geology remains dominated by Cretaceous strata, with the landscape transitioning from river valley pockets to more open, elevated slopes.
- Basalt-capped mesas lie beyond the trail corridor to the south, though the route itself stays primarily within shale and sandstone terrain.

## Day 6 — Santa Claus to Baldy Town
- The climb into Baldy Town marks a major geologic transition.
- As the trail ascends, the valley walls become steeper and the rocks more resistant, reflecting transition toward the Cimarron Range core.
- Baldy Town at 9,851 ft sits near older metamorphic and intrusive rocks, with the ridge country shaped by these hard, erosion-resistant units.

## Day 7 — Baldy Town layover
- The layover at Baldy Town provides an opportunity to observe the high-country geology directly.
- This area is part of the core of the Cimarron Range, where crystalline rocks such as amphibolite, hornblende schist, and pink to reddish pegmatite are common.
- Quartz-monzonite porphyry dikes and other intrusive bodies cut the high ground and help form the rugged ridges and sharp landforms.

## Day 8 — Baldy Town to Pueblano
- Descending from Baldy Town toward Pueblano takes hikers from the crystalline core back into more mixed terrain.
- The route moves through a transition zone where resistant dike ridges and metamorphic bedrock give way to softer sedimentary slopes.
- Pueblano lies in a valley area where the geology reflects both the uplifted mountain front and the more subdued creek-side country.

## Day 9 — Pueblano to Elkhorn
- Elkhorn Trail Camp is situated on ridge-top terrain with broad views of layered sedimentary and volcanic-influenced rocks.
- The geology here emphasizes the sculpted high-country slopes of the Cimarron Range, with evidence of the porphyry dike system and the underlying shale sequence.
- Conservation and trail construction work in this area will be set against the backdrop of these resistant rock masses.

## Day 10 — Elkhorn to Ponil
- This day descends into the Ponil valley and lower country.
- The trail moves from upland ridge geology into the broad river valley underlain by shale, sandstone, and alluvial deposits.
- Ponil camp is located near valley fill and meadowland, formed by long-term erosion of the surrounding sedimentary rocks.

## Day 11 — Ponil to Indian Writings
- The Indian Writings segment approaches Hart Peak and the archaeological area.
- Local outcrops are likely to include more resistant sandstones and shales, with visible cliffs and rock faces that preserve cultural markings.
- This day is a good example of the boundary between the Cimarron Range’s higher structural terrain and the lower sedimentary foothills.

## Day 12 — Indian Writings to Camping Headquarters
- The final leg returns through lower foothills to Six Mile Gate and back to Camping HQ.
- The observable geology returns to Cretaceous shale and softer valley slopes, with the final descent crossing the same broad sedimentary plains seen at the start of the trek.
- The loop closes where the low-elevation Great Plains margin meets the forested mountain front.
